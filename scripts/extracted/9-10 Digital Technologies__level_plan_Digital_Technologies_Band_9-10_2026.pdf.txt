Level description Context and cohort considerations
By the end of Year 10 students should have had the opportunity to apply computational thinking by defining and decomposing real-world problems, creating user experiences, designing and
modifying algorithms, and implementing them, including in an object-oriented programming language. Students use techniques, including interviewing stakeholders to develop user stories,
to increase the precision of their problem definitions and solution specifications. They verify their solutions solve the problem by validating their algorithms, represented as flowcharts and
pseudocode, and using test cases to confirm the correctness of their solutions. Students develop their object-oriented programming skills, and apply them to develop, modify and debug
programs. They explain the importance of abstraction by representing online documents in terms of content, structure and presentation, as well as exploring simple data compression
techniques and comparing their effectiveness.
Students consolidate their skills in data acquisition and interpretation, cleaning and validating data to ensure it is accurate, consistent and domain appropriate. They model multidimensional
data in more complex spreadsheets and relational databases, filtering and querying it to give insights into its meaning, and to pose further questions or make conclusions. They visualise this
data in customisable ways, allowing greater exploration of trends and outliers to support or challenge their analyses.
Students apply design thinking by using divergent techniques to generate design ideas for user experiences and solutions. They filter and prototype these ideas, developing user stories and
applying design criteria based on current and future needs and enterprising opportunities, as well as their created user stories, and revise and further develop their preferred ideas based on
their analysis. Students extend on these design criteria and user stories to evaluate the enterprise opportunities and future impact of existing solutions.
Students consolidate their systems thinking by exploring how the hardware and software components of digital systems interact to manage, control and secure access to data. They
increasingly use advanced features of existing and emerging digital tools to create interactive content for a diverse audience. They explore simple tools that help plan tasks, timelines and
responsibilities for individual and collaborative projects. Students extend their knowledge of the importance of security by developing cyber security threat models and exploring an example
of a supply chain vulnerability. They critique the digital footprint created by existing systems and their own solutions by applying the Australian Privacy Principles.
In Digital Technologies, students should have frequent opportunities for authentic learning by making key connections to other learning areas.
The Years 9–10 cohort consists of a diverse group of boys
with a wide range of digital competencies, from students
with strong technical skills and personal interest in IT, to
those who require further support to confidently engage
with digital systems. Many students come from regional
or rural backgrounds, where access to structured digital
learning opportunities may have been limited. The program
is designed to build on prior knowledge while introducing
more advanced and applied skills relevant to future study and
employment. The curriculum supports the school’s priority to
equip students with transferable digital skills, critical thinking
abilities, and an understanding of ethical technology use in a
rapidly changing world.

Description In this unit, students will explore the foundational
technologies behind the internet and how websites
function. They will examine how digital systems
use hardware and software to manage and secure
access to data across networks. Through guided
investigation and hands-on tasks, students will
learn how websites are constructed by separating
content, structure, and presentation using HTML
and CSS. They will also be introduced to simple
data compression techniques and understand their
importance in efficient web communication. This
unit builds the knowledge and skills required for
designing and analysing secure, well-structured,
and efficient digital content online.
In this unit, students will investigate how
cybercriminals design online scams and how these
techniques can be identified and avoided. Through
guided analysis and controlled, ethical simulations,
students will explore common features of scam
websites, including deceptive design choices,
persuasive language, fake forms, and misleading
links. By constructing mock scam websites in a
safe learning environment, students will develop
an understanding of how attackers exploit human
behaviour rather than technical vulnerabilities.
Students will also examine protective strategies
such as recognising warning signs, verifying
sources, and applying safe online practices. This
unit builds students’ awareness of cybersecurity
risks and equips them with practical skills to
analyse, evaluate, and respond safely to online
threats.
In this unit, students will use micro:bit devices to
collect real-world data and investigate how digital
systems acquire, store, and process information.
Students will design algorithms using logical
operators to control data collection and represent
these algorithms using flowcharts and pseudocode.
Collected data will be transferred to spreadsheets,
where students will apply techniques to validate,
manage, analyse, and visualise data. Through
interactive analysis, students will identify trends and
outliers, draw conclusions, and make predictions.
This unit develops students’ understanding of data-
driven decision making and the role of algorithms in
automated data collection systems.
In this unit, students will design, develop, and
analyse digital games using emerging game
development platforms. Students will explore
how user experience, algorithms, and data-driven
decision making influence gameplay and player
engagement. Through hands-on development and
analytical tasks, students will prototype interactive
game systems, implement and debug modular
programs, and evaluate game designs against
user needs and design criteria. The unit also
introduces students to the use of data analysis and
logical reasoning to improve game balance and
functionality, developing skills relevant to game
design, software development, and interactive
media industries.

Description Students will complete a written exam that
assesses their understanding of how websites
function and how data is managed in networked
digital systems. The exam will include questions
on the roles of hardware and software in securing
and controlling access to data, the structure of web
documents using HTML and CSS, and basic data
compression techniques.
In this assignment, students will design and
prototype a simulated scam website to investigate
how online scams exploit users and digital systems.
Students will define the problem using stakeholder
input and user stories, then generate and evaluate
design solutions. They will represent the website
using content, structure, and presentation,
explore simple data compression techniques, and
collect, model, and analyse data related to scam
behaviours using spreadsheets or databases.
Students will collaborate using agile project
management tools to create interactive content,
develop cyber security threat models, and evaluate
solutions against design criteria. The assignment
also requires students to apply the Australian
Privacy Principles to examine data misuse and
propose strategies to protect digital footprints.
In this assessment, students will design and
implement a micro:bit program to collect sports
science–related data such as movement,
acceleration, reaction time, or step counts.
Students will represent their data collection
algorithm using flowcharts or pseudocode and
validate the program by testing outputs against
expected results. Collected data will be transferred
into a spreadsheet, where students will store,
validate, analyse, and visualise the data to identify
trends and outliers. Using this analysis, students
will draw conclusions and make predictions about
performance, demonstrating how algorithms and
data analysis are used to measure and evaluate
physical activity in sports science contexts.
In this assignment, students will design and
develop an original digital game using GDevelop.
Students will prototype the user experience of
their game by designing gameplay mechanics,
controls, feedback systems, and visual elements for
a target audience. They will generate, modify, and
communicate alternative design ideas, refining their
game through iterative development. Students will
implement, modify, and debug modular programs
using selected algorithms and event-based logic
to control gameplay behaviour. The completed
game will be evaluated against design criteria
and user stories, with students reflecting on
usability, engagement, potential future impact, and
opportunities for further development. Students will
also use advanced features of GDevelop to create
and communicate interactive content suitable for a
diverse audience.

decisions rather than subjective judgement.

Assessment 7 — The Smart Warehouse Solution
Term 3
Week Week 9
Description In this assessment, students act as Automation Engineers tasked with designing a prototype Autonomous Guided Vehicle (AGV) for a smart warehouse logistics environment. Students will decompose the complex
problem of autonomous warehouse operation into manageable sub-problems, object manipulation, and grid-based navigation.
Using an object-oriented programming language (MicroPython), students will implement, modify, and debug a modular control system that governs the robot’s behaviour. The robot must operate autonomously using
a finite state machine to manage transitions between behaviours such as searching, lifting, and returning. Students will apply algorithms to interpret sensor data and calculate movement within a coordinate grid,
demonstrating how hardware and software can be integrated to solve an authentic logistics challenge.

Assessment 8 — The Systems Control Folio
Term 4
Week Week 9
Description In this assessment, students act as Control Systems Engineers responsible for testing, calibrating, and optimising their autonomous robot. Students will investigate how sensors and motors interact with software by
designing and validating control algorithms, such as proportional (P) or PID control, to improve navigation accuracy and system stability.
Students will conduct repeated test runs, collect sensor telemetry data, and analyse this data using graphs to evaluate the effectiveness of their control logic. The folio will document algorithm validation through
before-and-after testing, showing how adjustments improve performance. Students will also include flowcharts representing their robot’s finite state machine and provide a written evaluation of the system’s limitations,
reliability, and readiness for use in an enterprise context.
